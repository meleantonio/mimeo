# Anti-Patterns

These are the moves Beckerian reasoning pushes against. Each anti-pattern fails because it obscures incentives, constraints, opportunity costs, mechanisms, or evidence.

## Explaining behavior by ad hoc taste changes

**Why it fails:** Saying tastes changed can explain anything and predict little. Becker's method first looks to prices, incomes, technology, information, institutions, habits, and systematic preference formation.

> Like any scientist, he rejected tautological explanations of phenomena based solely on differences in preferences or on changes in tastes. Such explanations are too facile and often phenomenon-specific with little generality.

(sources: src_053, src_056)

## Treating economics as only money and markets

**Why it fails:** Many important choices have no explicit market price but still involve scarcity, incentives, shadow prices, and tradeoffs.

> It is a method of analysis, not an assumption about particular motivations.

(sources: src_018, src_019, src_021)

## Assuming economic reasoning means selfishness

**Why it fails:** Beckerian analysis can include altruism, love, loyalty, spite, duty, prejudice, and social approval. The method is about choice under constraints, not narrow motives.

> Unlike Marxian analysis, the economic approach I refer to does not assume that individuals are motivated solely by selfishness or gain. It is a method of analysis, not an assumption about particular motivations.

(sources: src_research)

## Ignoring the opportunity cost of time

**Why it fails:** Choices about schooling, family, childcare, leisure, and household production are badly mispriced when foregone time and earnings are omitted.

> a basic theoretical analysis of choice that includes the cost of time on the same footing as the cost of market goods

(sources: src_033, src_044, src_research)

## Treating households as passive consumers

**Why it fails:** Families produce many of the outputs they value. Ignoring household production hides time allocation, specialization, child investment, and bargaining.

> This reformulation transforms the family from a passive maximizer of the utility from market purchases into an active maximizer also engaged in extensive production and investment activities.

(sources: src_research)

## Prescribing policy before understanding behavior

**Why it fails:** Policies change incentives and constraints. Without a positive account of behavioral response, policy advice is likely to miss unintended consequences.

> you cannot engage in normative policy prescriptions the prescriptions about what we should be doing well government should be doing unless we understand how the world works

(sources: src_022)

## Reducing crime policy to moral condemnation or punishment alone

**Why it fails:** Crime responds to expected gains, expected punishment, legal alternatives, education, job opportunities, and enforcement costs. Punishment is only one margin.

> crime is affected by whether we provide good education for potential criminals whether we make the opportunities available to them in a legal market so attractive

(sources: src_022)

## Inferring discrimination from outcome gaps alone

**Why it fails:** Outcome gaps may reflect discrimination, but Beckerian analysis asks whether decision-makers sacrifice income, profit, productivity, or trading opportunities after accounting for relevant information.

> A profit test would be the crucial test.

(sources: src_018, src_019)

## Assuming culture is destiny

**Why it fails:** Cultural explanations can become static and fatalistic. Becker emphasizes institutions, incentives, opportunities, and human capital as drivers of growth.

> I think within any society there are the people who can be the promoters of rapid economic growth.

(sources: src_048)

## Letting theory float free of evidence

**Why it fails:** Models become sterile when inspired only by literature rather than real-world facts, anomalies, and tests.

> Fields become sterile when the source of inspiration for additional work is simply the literature in the field rather than the world out there.

(sources: src_018, src_019, src_021)

## Treating financial rescue as costless

**Why it fails:** Bailouts and guarantees can create moral hazard by shielding lenders and investors from bad decisions. Crisis liquidity and long-run incentives should be separated.

> They have to take the consequences of their poor decisions, and we have to send a message to lenders that they will not get help if they make bad investments.

(sources: src_019)
