# Frameworks

Use these frameworks to translate Beckerian ideas into concrete analytical steps. They are designed for advice, critiques, research design, policy evaluation, and decision support.

## Economic approach to human behavior

**Canonical statement:** Explain behavior by modeling agents as choosing under preferences, constraints, prices, shadow prices, and equilibrium conditions.

**Rationale:** This is the general Beckerian framework for nonmarket as well as market behavior. It is useful when a problem is framed as moral, cultural, emotional, criminal, familial, or sociological but still involves tradeoffs.

**Steps:**
1. Identify agents and choices.
2. Specify objectives broadly.
3. Identify constraints, prices, risks, time costs, and opportunity costs.
4. Include implicit or shadow prices where no market price exists.
5. Predict comparative statics: what changes when a constraint or price changes?
6. Compare the prediction with evidence.

> Economics is a method that gives the social scientist the necessary tools to understand and even transform the world that surrounds him/her.

(sources: src_056, src_027, src_053, src_057, src_017, src_028, src_022, src_research, src_054, src_006, src_001)

## Human capital analysis

**Canonical statement:** Analyze activities that increase skills, knowledge, health, habits, or values as investments embodied in people.

**Rationale:** This framework is central for education, training, career moves, health, parenting, migration, and labor-market outcomes.

**Steps:**
1. Identify the investment activity.
2. Ask what future capacity or welfare it may raise.
3. Count direct outlays, effort, time, risk, and foregone earnings.
4. Estimate future monetary and nonmonetary returns.
5. Discount returns and compare with alternatives.
6. Consider ability, family background, selection, and who captures the return.

> Benefits include cultural and other non-monetary gains along with improvement in earnings and occupations, while costs usually depend mainly on the foregone value of the time spent on these investments.

(sources: src_048, src_047, src_018, src_021, src_049, src_046, src_research, src_045)

## Household production and allocation of time

**Canonical statement:** Model households as producers that combine market goods and differentiated time inputs to produce commodities that generate utility.

**Rationale:** This explains consumption, childcare, fertility, meals, leisure, health, home production, and labor supply better than a model that treats households as passive buyers.

**Steps:**
1. Identify the household output being produced.
2. List goods, time, skills, and knowledge inputs.
3. Price time through wages or shadow wages.
4. Distinguish goods-intensive from time-intensive activities.
5. Analyze substitution among goods, time, and specialization.
6. Consider bargaining power when household members differ.

> The household is assumed to produce and consume a vector of commodities ⁠ . These commodities are associated with different levels of activities performed by the household (e.g. consumption of food, child‐rearing, leisure activities), including leisure on the job

(sources: src_033, src_044, src_039, src_017, src_006)

## Economics of the family

**Canonical statement:** Analyze marriage, divorce, fertility, division of labor, and child investment through matching, specialization, bargaining, production, and intergenerational investment.

**Rationale:** Family choices are emotional and economic. Beckerian analysis allows love and obligation to matter while still asking how wages, education, time costs, policy, and expected welfare shape decisions.

**Steps:**
1. Identify household members and their resources.
2. Analyze gains from matching, specialization, and shared production.
3. Model fertility and child investment as quantity-quality tradeoffs.
4. Account for divorce, bargaining power, and outside options.
5. Trace intergenerational human-capital effects.

> Becker’s theory of marriage posits that individuals enter into marital unions based on economic considerations, such as the division of labor, sharing of resources, and investment in children’s upbringing.

(sources: src_033, src_037, src_039, src_044)

## Economic model of crime and optimal enforcement

**Canonical statement:** Analyze crime as a choice under expected gains, expected punishment, legal alternatives, and enforcement costs.

**Rationale:** This framework turns crime policy into a deterrence and social-cost problem rather than a purely moral or pathological diagnosis.

**Steps:**
1. Estimate gains from the offense.
2. Estimate legal alternatives and opportunity costs.
3. Estimate probability of apprehension, conviction, and punishment severity.
4. Predict response to changes in detection, sanctions, or legal opportunities.
5. Compare crime harms with policing, trial, punishment, and error costs.

> A person commits an offense if the expected utility to him exceeds the utility he could get by using his time and other resources at other activities.

(sources: src_033, src_037, src_039)

## Taste-based discrimination

**Canonical statement:** Model discrimination as a preference against interaction with a group, measured by willingness to sacrifice income, profits, productivity, or opportunities.

**Rationale:** This framework separates prejudice from mere outcome gaps and asks when discriminatory tastes survive competition or institutional constraints.

**Steps:**
1. Identify whose taste for discrimination matters: employer, worker, customer, lender, voter, or state.
2. Translate prejudice into a cost or discrimination coefficient.
3. Ask what the discriminator gives up.
4. Compare outcomes after accounting for productivity, risk, information, and selection.
5. Analyze competition, entry, monopoly, politics, and institutions.

> A profit test would be the crucial test.

(sources: src_018, src_019)

## Mechanism-based policy analysis

**Canonical statement:** Policy analysis should identify the mechanisms generating a problem and design interventions around behavioral responses.

**Rationale:** Intentions do not determine outcomes. Taxes, subsidies, bans, prices, queues, entitlements, and punishments alter margins of behavior.

**Steps:**
1. State the policy goal.
2. Identify the current mechanism producing the problem.
3. Identify the margins people can adjust.
4. Predict intended and unintended responses.
5. Compare administrative costs, enforcement costs, distributional effects, and welfare effects.
6. Use evidence to revise the mechanism.

> Becker approached the study of public policy by attempting to understand the underlying mechanisms generating the problems studied and by using this knowledge to suggest effective policies.

(sources: src_053, src_055, src_056)

## Theory-data arbitrage

**Canonical statement:** Keep theory and empirical work in continuous dialogue; use evidence to inspire models and models to generate testable implications.

**Rationale:** This guards against both empty abstraction and atheoretical empiricism.

**Steps:**
1. Start from a real-world regularity or puzzle.
2. Build a simple model with clear assumptions.
3. Derive predictions beyond the original case.
4. Test across groups, time, and settings.
5. Revise the theory or measurement when facts resist it.

> But you need many people arbitraging between the empirical and theoretical sides. Unless you have that, I believe fields become sterile.

(sources: src_018, src_019, src_021)
