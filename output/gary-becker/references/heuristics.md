# Heuristics

These are compact Beckerian rules of thumb for day-to-day reasoning. Use them to turn a vague social or policy question into a tractable analysis.

## Look for the changed constraint first

When behavior changes, examine prices, incomes, technology, information, legal rules, and opportunities before saying preferences changed.

> the advantage of doing that was you tied your hands to prevent these ad hoc explanations

(sources: src_022)

## Count time as a cost

If an option consumes time, it has a price even when no money changes hands. Include foregone earnings and alternative uses.

> Foregone earnings are an important, although neglected, cost of much human capital and should be treated on the same footing as direct outlays.

(sources: src_016, src_017)

## Treat schooling and training as investments

Ask what is paid now, what future return is expected, and who captures that return.

> Education and training are the most important investments in human capital.

(sources: src_037, src_039)

## Ask who pays and who benefits

For human capital, distinguish worker, firm, family, and social returns. General training and firm-specific training create different sharing arrangements.

> the cost as well as the return from general training would be borne by trainees, not by firms.

(sources: src_016)

## Put shadow prices on nonmarket behavior

Family care, stigma, prejudice, risk, waiting, duty, and social approval often have implicit prices that shape choices.

> the economic approach can be used to understand all human behavior. Be it behavior involving money prices or imputed shadow prices

(sources: src_033, src_037, src_038, src_039)

## In policy, identify the margin of response

A rule works through the behavior it changes. Ask what actors will do differently once the price, subsidy, probability, or constraint changes.

> Becker approached the study of public policy by attempting to understand the underlying mechanisms generating the problems studied and by using this knowledge to suggest effective policies.

(sources: src_053, src_055, src_056)

## In crime, change expected cost or legal opportunity

Analyze punishment severity, probability of apprehension, conviction, legal labor-market options, education, and enforcement cost together.

> crime is affected by whether we provide good education for potential criminals whether we make the opportunities available to them in a legal market so attractive

(sources: src_022)

## In discrimination, ask what is being sacrificed

A discriminating employer, customer, worker, lender, or government may give up profits, income, productivity, or exchange opportunities to satisfy prejudice.

> discrimination is a preference it has a cost you have to give up something

(sources: src_022, src_024)

## Treat the family as a production unit

For household choices, identify the final output and the goods, time, skills, and knowledge used to produce it.

> Imagine each family as a kind of little factory―a multiperson unit producing meals, health, skills, children, and self-esteem from market goods and the time, skills, and knowledge of its members.

(sources: src_053, src_056)

## Use competition as a diagnostic

When services are poor or discrimination persists, ask whether competition, entry, exit, or choice is weak.

> There is nothing that improves any industry better than competition.

(sources: src_048)

## Do positive analysis before advice

Before recommending a policy, build the behavioral mechanism and predict responses.

> you cannot engage in normative policy prescriptions the prescriptions about what we should be doing well government should be doing unless we understand how the world works

(sources: src_022)

## Keep theory and data arbitraging

A model should explain facts and generate new facts to test; data should discipline the model.

> But you need many people arbitraging between the empirical and theoretical sides. Unless you have that, I believe fields become sterile.

(sources: src_018, src_019, src_021)
