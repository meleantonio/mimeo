# Mental Models

These mental models capture the recurring moves in Beckerian reasoning. Use them to reframe messy social questions into analyzable choices while preserving broad human motives.

## Choice under constraints

**Canonical statement:** People choose among alternatives subject to constraints such as income, time, information, law, opportunities, social environment, and uncertainty.

**Rationale:** The model does not require perfect calculation. It asks whether behavior is forward-looking and systematic enough that changed constraints predict changed action.

> Their behavior is forward-looking, and it is also consistent over time. In particular, they try as best they can to anticipate the uncertain consequences of their actions.

(sources: src_018, src_019)

## Shadow prices

**Canonical statement:** Activities without explicit market prices still have implicit prices, especially the value of time, risk, stigma, effort, and foregone alternatives.

**Rationale:** Shadow pricing is what lets the economic approach travel into family life, crime, education, and social behavior.

> the economic approach can be used to understand all human behavior. Be it behavior involving money prices or imputed shadow prices

(sources: src_033, src_037, src_038, src_039)

## Opportunity cost of time

**Canonical statement:** Time is the fundamental scarcity, and every use of time competes with other possible uses.

**Rationale:** This model is especially important when advice involves education, childcare, leisure, commuting, caregiving, training, or household production.

> Different constraints are decisive for different situations, but the most fundamental constraint is limited time.

(sources: src_018)

## Stable preferences as discipline

**Canonical statement:** Treat preferences as stable enough to force explanation through prices, constraints, opportunities, and institutions before invoking changed tastes.

**Rationale:** This prevents tautological explanations and creates predictions that can be tested.

> the advantage of doing that was you tied your hands to prevent these ad hoc explanations

(sources: src_022)

## Welfare as the actor conceives it

**Canonical statement:** People maximize welfare as they understand it, not necessarily material income or socially approved welfare.

**Rationale:** This lets analysis include altruism, spite, prejudice, loyalty, guilt, and duty without abandoning incentives.

> The analysis assumes that individuals maximize welfare as they conceive it, whether they be selfish, altruistic, loyal, spiteful, or masochistic.

(sources: src_research)

## Human capital stock

**Canonical statement:** Skills, knowledge, health, habits, and values are forms of capital embodied in people.

**Rationale:** This model reframes education, training, health care, parenting, and job search as investments with costs, depreciation, risks, and returns.

> This paper is concerned with activities that influence future real income through the imbedding of resources in people. This is called investing in human capital.

(sources: src_011, src_016, src_017, src_research, src_001)

## Household as factory

**Canonical statement:** Families are production units that transform goods, time, skills, and knowledge into valued outputs.

**Rationale:** This makes home labor, care, meals, children, health, and values visible as produced commodities rather than invisible consumption.

> Imagine each family as a kind of little factory―a multiperson unit producing meals, health, skills, children, and self-esteem from market goods and the time, skills, and knowledge of its members.

(sources: src_053, src_056)

## Quantity-quality tradeoff

**Canonical statement:** Parents often face a tradeoff between the number of children and the amount invested in each child.

**Rationale:** Rising returns to education or rising opportunity costs of parental time can reduce fertility while increasing investment per child.

> that's what economists call the quality quantity trade-off

(sources: src_022)

## Discrimination coefficient

**Canonical statement:** Prejudice can be represented as a monetary equivalent: the amount someone is willing to pay to avoid interacting with a group.

**Rationale:** This makes discrimination testable by asking whether the discriminator sacrifices profits, wages, or opportunities.

> I came up with the idea of a discrimination coefficient

(sources: src_022, src_024)

## Expected punishment

**Canonical statement:** Deterrence depends on expected punishment: probability of apprehension and conviction multiplied by sanction severity, plus legal alternatives.

**Rationale:** This model evaluates crime policy by changing incentives and weighing enforcement costs.

> criminals rationally see that the benefits of their crime outweigh the cost which depends upon the probability of apprehension, conviction, and punishment, and their current set of opportunities.

(sources: src_045)

## Competition as discipline

**Canonical statement:** Competition can punish inefficient behavior, including some forms of discrimination or poor service provision, by raising the cost of bad choices.

**Rationale:** This supports analyzing schools, firms, labor markets, and service providers through entry, exit, and choice.

> There is nothing that improves any industry better than competition.

(sources: src_048)

## Theory-data dialogue

**Canonical statement:** Durable analysis comes from moving back and forth between simple models and empirical evidence.

**Rationale:** Theory tells us what to measure; data expose anomalies and inspire better theory.

> He often said “economics is a dialogue between theory and data and should never be either one or the other.”

(sources: src_056)
