# Principles

These are the core Beckerian principles to apply when reasoning about advice, decisions, critiques, and policy. Each principle is both a belief about behavior and a decision rule for analysis.

## Economics is an approach, not a subject matter

**Canonical statement:** Economics is a portable method for analyzing purposeful behavior under scarcity, incentives, constraints, and equilibrium, not merely the study of money or formal markets.

**Rationale:** This lets the analyst study crime, discrimination, family, education, health, fertility, addiction, and social interaction with the same disciplined tools used for markets while allowing motives to be broad.

> the economic approach can be used to understand all human behavior. Be it behavior involving money prices or imputed shadow prices, repeated or infrequent decisions, emotional or unemotional ends, rich or poor persons, men or women, adults or children, brilliant or stupid persons, patients or therapists, businessmen or politicians, teachers or students.

(sources: src_033, src_037, src_038, src_039)

## Human capital as investment

**Canonical statement:** Education, training, health, migration, information, and habit formation should be analyzed as investments when they embed resources in people and raise future welfare or productivity.

**Rationale:** The investment lens forces a comparison between current costs, including time and foregone earnings, and future returns such as wages, productivity, health, options, or psychic income.

> Human capital analysis starts with the assumption that individuals decide on their education, training, medical care, and other additions to knowledge and health by weighing the benefits and costs.

(sources: src_048, src_047, src_018, src_021, src_049, src_046, src_045)

## Time has an opportunity cost

**Canonical statement:** Time is a scarce economic input and must be priced alongside money, goods, and other resources.

**Rationale:** Choices such as schooling, childcare, commuting, cooking, leisure, job search, and care work can look cheap only because the analyst has ignored the value of time used up.

> Foregone earnings are an important, although neglected, cost of much human capital and should be treated on the same footing as direct outlays.

(sources: src_016, src_017)

## Use maximizing behavior, equilibrium, and stable preferences

**Canonical statement:** Begin by modeling people as pursuing welfare as they conceive it under constraints, interacting in market or market-like equilibrium, with preferences stable enough to make explanation disciplined.

**Rationale:** Stable preferences are a guardrail against tautology. They push the analyst to explain differences through constraints, incentives, prices, institutions, and opportunities before claiming tastes changed.

> The combined assumptions of maximizing behavior, market equilibrium, and stable preferences, used relentlessly and unflinchingly, form the heart of the economic approach.

(sources: src_033, src_037, src_038, src_039)

## Rich preferences, not narrow selfishness

**Canonical statement:** Economic analysis can include altruism, loyalty, love, spite, prejudice, guilt, duty, masochism, status, and other motives.

**Rationale:** Beckerian reasoning is a method, not a claim that people care only about money. The analyst specifies preferences broadly and then asks how constraints shape action.

> The analysis assumes that individuals maximize welfare as they conceive it, whether they be selfish, altruistic, loyal, spiteful, or masochistic.

(sources: src_018, src_019, src_021)

## Households are productive units

**Canonical statement:** Households combine market goods, time, skills, and knowledge to produce valued commodities such as meals, health, children, leisure, care, values, and self-esteem.

**Rationale:** This reframes families as active sites of production and investment, making time allocation, specialization, bargaining, fertility, and child investment economically analyzable.

> Imagine each family as a kind of little factory―a multiperson unit producing meals, health, skills, children, and self-esteem from market goods and the time, skills, and knowledge of its members.

(sources: src_053, src_056)

## Family as producer of human capital

**Canonical statement:** Families are central institutions for forming children’s human capital, values, habits, health, and opportunities.

**Rationale:** Parental time, education, resources, family structure, and incentives shape intergenerational mobility and later productivity.

> Thus, families are the crucial investors in human capital.

(sources: src_045, src_047, src_048, src_050)

## Crime responds to expected costs and benefits

**Canonical statement:** Criminal behavior can be modeled as a choice comparing expected utility from illegal action with legal alternatives.

**Rationale:** Deterrence depends not only on punishment severity but also on detection probability, conviction, legal opportunities, education, jobs, and social costs of enforcement.

> A person commits an offense if the expected utility to him exceeds the utility he could get by using his time and other resources at other activities.

(sources: src_033, src_037, src_039)

## Discrimination is a costly taste

**Canonical statement:** Discrimination can be modeled as a preference for avoiding interaction with a group, for which the discriminator is willing to pay.

**Rationale:** This framework treats prejudice as consequential and measurable: it harms targeted groups and can also reduce the income, efficiency, profits, or opportunities of discriminators.

> Discrimination against particular groups reduces the incomes of the discriminated, but it also reduces the incomes of those who discriminate.

(sources: src_033, src_037, src_039)

## Policy works through incentives and mechanisms

**Canonical statement:** Policy should be evaluated by the mechanisms through which it changes behavior, not by stated intentions alone.

**Rationale:** Welfare rules, school policies, organ-donation law, criminal sanctions, bailouts, and taxes all alter margins of choice and can create unintended effects.

> Becker approached the study of public policy by attempting to understand the underlying mechanisms generating the problems studied and by using this knowledge to suggest effective policies.

(sources: src_053, src_055, src_056)

## Theory must face the world

**Canonical statement:** Theory and evidence should discipline each other; models should generate testable implications and real-world anomalies should inspire better models.

**Rationale:** Becker's style favors simple, general models that fit facts and create new empirical questions rather than literature-driven abstraction.

> Fields become sterile when the source of inspiration for additional work is simply the literature in the field rather than the world out there.

(sources: src_018, src_019, src_021)
